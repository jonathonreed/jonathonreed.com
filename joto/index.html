The Joto

The Why & the How

My photo archive is littered with photos I call ‘jotos,’ a word I made up in reference to my specific style of jump shot.

I’ve taken ‘the joto’ windswept on the edge of a cliff in Norway, ankle-deep in the Bali Sea in Indonesia, sunlit above the Grand Canyon in the United States. It’s become a go-to shot when I find myself in a scenic landscape. Primarily for the fun of it. It’s sort of like going down a slide when I was a kid: it doesn’t really serve much of a purpose, it’s just fun to do. In 2015 I did parkour training and came away with a heightened love for the practice of controlled risk. It involves preparation and progression, and definitely muscle, which has its own sense of fun. In the same sense of parkour, I like taking any opportunity to challenge other people’s definition of what is safe. I think most people—kids especially—are too physically safe and it’s good to push that boundary. Lastly, let’s be honest, it’s a glorified selfie. How do I take a picture of myself and make it not look self-centered? Make it look epic.

One of my mottos is based on a quote that I saw beside an outdoor basketball court: “We don’t stop playing because we grow older. We grow older because we stop playing.” So that’s the joto. Playing.

❮ ❯

I encourage you to find your own unique thing. If you want to try the joto, though, here’s how I get the shot.

If I’m out with someone, odds are I’ll rope them into being my photographer. Depending on their skill level with a DSLR it usually takes a few tries. I’ve also set up my own shots with my camera on a tripod and either an intervalometer or an audio trigger run through Magic Lantern.

I usually go for a centered composition. This probably goes without saying but the photo is best captured at the peak of the jump. Takes a bit of practice but allows for the best composition and, most importantly, the best air. That’s important because for me, the crucial element of the photo is my body clearing the horizon. I usually position the camera at a low angle with a wide-angle lens, making the most of perspective and lens distortion to make it look like I’m jumping even higher than I am. Plus I can jump quite high. But the position and lens are definitely part of it.

Those are the general practices. Center/peak, low/wide-angle. I break those rules if I feel like it will make for a better photo but generally that’s what I go for.
