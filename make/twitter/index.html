Twitter

Intro

Before we start, you should know that this is a relatively subjective explanation of a relatively complex and ever-changing web platform. I use Twitter in a pretty specific way that works for me, but all kinds of people use it in all kinds of different ways. I'm not an expert, but I keep an eye on technological developments—especially in social media. So I'm sharing what I know in the hopes that it will help you find the way you want to use Twitter.

The basics

Username. My username, for example, is @jonathoncomfort. When you're writing a tweet, anything that is preceded by the @ symbol automatically becomes a link to that Twitter account, and that person gets a notification. If you start a tweet with a username, it only appears in the Tweets & replies stream on your profile.

In this case I tweeted the username @younggreenscan because I was having a conversation with that Twitter account.

@younggreenscan Word! Looking forward to working with @KeenanMThomas for @QueensUGreens this fall. #GPC #cdnpoli #elxn42

— Jonathon Reed (@jonathoncomfort) May 6, 2015
In this case I tweeted the username @MikeHudema because he wrote the article, and he has a large Twitter audience, so I wanted him to see that I had shared his writing. You can see it doesn't have the same conversational element, but it did get his attention.

We've had enough of #Harper working for #oil companies instead of our collective future. http://t.co/SpYc2E7yOE @MikeHudema #tarsands #NoTPP

— Jonathon Reed (@jonathoncomfort) April 24, 2015
Some people put a period in front of the username at the start of the tweet in order to ensure it stays in the main Twitter stream. I don't do this but it is acceptable Twitter grammar.

.@TIME has chosen 350's @mayboeve as a "next generation leader" - we may be biased, but we think she's great too http://t.co/jik8jkuUSz

— 350 dot org (@350) May 29, 2015

Hashtag. Any word in a tweet that is preceded by a # symbol becomes a link to a collection of all the tweets that include that hashtag. An example would be the hashtag #cdnpoli. Hashtags can be general words like #climate or specific terms like #elxn42. You can't use symbols in hashtags.

In this case I used four different hashtags to connect my tweet to four different national conversations: climate change, the tar sands industry, the NDP and the Alberta election. Hashtags can be either in the body of the tweet or added onto the end.

Time to push for action on #climatechange: Alberta #tarsands now in uncharted waters with #NDP premier http://t.co/shMmsuj31Y #abvote

— Jonathon Reed (@jonathoncomfort) May 6, 2015
Some hashtags are very current and refer to specific campaigns or movements. In this case, the direct action in Seattle pushed the hashtag #ShellNo to gain and consolidate media attention. It's sometimes hard to figure out which hashtag would be related to the issue you're tweeting about; my only advice would be to look around on other people's Twitter pages or in online news.

'#ShellNo': Hundreds take to boats in Seattle to protest Arctic drilling - next is the #shellagm http://t.co/IPNTkTo7Na

— UK Tar Sands Network (@NoTarSands) May 17, 2015
