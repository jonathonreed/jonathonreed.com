Twitter

Intro

Before we start, you should know that this is a relatively subjective explanation of a relatively complex and ever-changing web platform. I use Twitter in a pretty specific way that works for me, but all kinds of people use it in all kinds of different ways. I'm not an expert, but I keep an eye on technological developments—especially in social media. So I'm sharing what I know in the hopes that it will help you find the way you want to use Twitter.

Terminology

Username. My username, for example, is @jonathoncomfort. When you're writing a tweet, anything that is preceded by the @ symbol automatically becomes a link to that Twitter account, and that person gets a notification. If you start a tweet with a username, it only appears in the Tweets & replies stream on your profile.

In this case I tweeted the username @younggreenscan because I was having a conversation with that Twitter account.

@younggreenscan Word! Looking forward to working with @KeenanMThomas for @QueensUGreens this fall. #GPC #cdnpoli #elxn42

— Jonathon Reed (@jonathoncomfort) May 6, 2015

Performance. It's not just poetry, right, it's meant to be performed. So I always have body language associated with my words or interactions with the audience.

And you, the audience, really you this time:
I didn't say it to them so I'm slamming it to you

What determines my language

Alliteration. I like to place words that start with the same letter together because of the way it lets sentences easily roll off my tongue. I use it to keep my lines tight and fitting together nicely.

So here I am spilling some secrets into a microphone;
Saying them now because someone told me
That somehow late is better than never
So I'm sorry

Rhyme. I rhyme kind of by accident. I used to hate rhyming poetry, because I felt that it constricted my word choice. So now when I do rhyme, I don't let it get in the way of what I want to say, you know? But it's easier than I thought it was and I've found that carefully choosing words that rhyme so that sentences balance can have a really cool effect.

I knocked him down
He hit the ground and he didn't make a sound
Until I heard a teacher say, "Are you okay?"
And he whispered, "I want Jonathon."

Rhythm. The piece has got to flow, and I can't think of a better way to explain it. Syntax, syllables, that kind of thing. For tempo, I like to juxtapose speaking fast/hard and slow/soft. Practicing a lot lets me be able to speak some parts really fast, which then makes the slow parts stand out even more. Feeling the rhythm of the piece helps make sure the fast parts are still understandable.

[fast] Don't cry, toughen up, be a man
What are you some kind of faggot, you faggot
You mama's boy, what are you gonna go home and cry about it
Come on walk it off, rub some dirt in it, be a good soldier
[slow] Be a good soldier

Imagery. I'm not sure if this is worth including, but I try to be really intentional about the descriptive language I use. Metaphors can be awesome awesome awesome for conveying an idea really vividly. Sometimes I bring up the same metaphor to slam the message home.

And you know what that felt like?
It felt like an ocean wave slamming against me
An entire tide of salt water rising behind my eyes
[...]
I want you to imagine
That you have a chance to reach out and grab my friend's fist
Right before that ocean wave hit

As far as I go with metaphors, though, I also want to make sure that what I'm describing is really clear. So sometimes I just lay it out really excplicitly.

To the boy who tried smoking because it made his mom mad and he's gonna die anyway
Who counts his ribs and joints and tries hard to believe in that thing that his pastor calls hope
Who locks his fingers together like puzzle piece prayers as he falls asleep:
I wish I could take your cancer and hold it deep inside me

What inspires me

Some of my favourite slam poets that are kind of famous online are Shane Koyczan, Sarah Kay, Andrea Gibson and Lacey Roop. I've also jammed with some other talented artists like Luke Nephew and Ritalin.

I have a playlist on YouTube of my favourite slam poems. I think it was the default slam poetry playlist or something because it has 60,000+ views. Anyways. It goes without saying but they're all incredible. I suggest watching them and then hitting up the recommended videos in the sidebar.

What I want you to keep in mind most of all

Slam poetry is whatever the hell you want it to be. I just wrote a bunch of stuff that I made up, right. There are no rules, and if there were, I'd tell you to break them. I was thinking this could be helpful for you to get started and to build confidence.

Have fun. Be real. Looking forward to spitting with you.
